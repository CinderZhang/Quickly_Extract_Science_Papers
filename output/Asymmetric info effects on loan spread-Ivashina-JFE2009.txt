Q: Can you give me a very clear explanation of the core assertions, implications, and mechanics elucidated in this paper?

A: This paper by Victoria Ivashina investigates the cost implications of information asymmetry between the lead bank and members of a lending syndicate in the syndicated loan market. 

The core assertions of the paper are:

1. Information Asymmetry: In a lending syndicate, the lead bank retains only a fraction of the loan but acts as the intermediary between the borrower and the syndicate participants. This creates an information asymmetry, as the lead bank has more information about the borrower's creditworthiness than the syndicate participants.

2. Adverse Selection and Moral Hazard: The lead bank's information advantage can lead to adverse selection and moral hazard problems. Adverse selection occurs when the lead bank has an incentive to syndicate lower-quality loans. Moral hazard arises when the lead bank's incentive to monitor the borrower decreases after syndicating the loan.

3. Ownership and Loan Spread: The paper suggests that an increase in the lead bank's ownership of the loan should reduce information asymmetry and the related premium, thereby reducing the overall loan spread. However, a larger ownership also increases the lead bank's credit risk exposure, which could increase the loan spread.

The paper uses a novel instrument to isolate the effects of information asymmetry and diversification on loan spreads. This instrument measures each loan's contribution to the credit risk of the lead bank's loan portfolio. 

The implications of the paper are significant. It finds that information asymmetry within the lending syndicate accounts for approximately 4% of the total credit cost. This suggests that reducing information asymmetry could lead to lower borrowing costs in the syndicated loan market.
